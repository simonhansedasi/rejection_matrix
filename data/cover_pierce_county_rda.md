# Cover Letter — Pierce County Research and Data Analyst

---

The hardest part of a policy analysis project is not the model — it is knowing which findings a county planner can actually act on. Last year I built a transit equity analysis for Snohomish County: frequency-weighted accessibility scores, ACS vulnerability indices, and a gap score ranking census block groups by unmet need. The technical output was a pandana gravity model with demographic weighting. The useful output was a one-page map and a priority ranking a regional agency could put in front of a board. I am more interested in the second deliverable than the first.

That project reflects how I approach quantitative work: rigorous methods in service of a decision someone else has to make. My MSc in Geophysical Data Science trained me in a field where ground truth is rarely clean and uncertainty quantification is part of the answer, not a footnote — I have carried that discipline into public sector equity analysis, a re-identification risk tool built on Census microdata, and a peer-reviewed publication in the Journal of Glaciology. The instinct to ask "is this source valid, is this method appropriate, is this finding actionable" is the same whether the subject is glacier ice volume or a behavioral health program cohort.

Pierce County's Research and Data Analysis team is doing the kind of work that interests me most: analysis embedded directly in budget and program decisions, with audiences ranging from departmental leads to the County Council to community members. The Finance and Enterprise Services framing — countywide scope, strong stewardship mandate — aligns well with my background, which spans financial operations at the University of Washington, production data infrastructure, and demographic data tools built with data validity as a design constraint. I have built for public audiences before, and I know that getting the analysis right is a prerequisite for getting it heard.

I would welcome the opportunity to talk through the role.
