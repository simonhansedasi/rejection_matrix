## Q2: Describe a time when you used data not only to analyze a problem, but to shape strategy, influence decision-making, or drive organizational change.

At Randy's World-Wide, my job title was Accounts Receivable Specialist, but the actual work was data recovery and triage. The company's outstanding receivables were a mess — payments applied to the wrong invoices, short pays that had gone unnoticed, duplicate payments sitting unapplied, customer accounts with years of accumulated discrepancies. The raw data existed but had never been systematically analyzed.

I built pipelines to parse and structure the transaction history, then designed an aging and anomaly analysis that scored each account by a combination of dollar exposure, payment pattern irregularity, and time since last resolution. The output wasn't just a report on what was wrong — it was a prioritized list: here is where to direct collections effort first, here is what can wait, here is what needs escalation versus routine follow-up.

The collections and finance teams used that prioritization directly to decide who to call, in what order, and with what information in hand. Accounts that had been sitting unresolved for months were resolved within weeks once the effort was directed by data rather than intuition or squeaky-wheel urgency. The analysis changed how the team allocated its time.

The same pattern runs through my transit vulnerability work in Snohomish County. The surface question was where transit service was missing. The strategic question was where missing service constituted genuine community harm — where the populations most dependent on transit were also most concentrated in coverage gaps. That reframing, from "where is the gap" to "where does the gap hurt the most," is what turns analysis into a planning tool. A map of coverage gaps tells you what exists. A vulnerability index tells you where to act first.

In both cases: data in service of a decision, not a document.

## Q3: Tell us about a time you partnered with stakeholders who had different levels of data literacy. How did you navigate that collaboration?

At Randy's World-Wide, I worked alongside a collections and finance team whose relationship to data was entirely concrete: they thought in dollars, not rates. When I brought an aging analysis that expressed account health in terms of ratios, percentages, and payment deviation scores, the response was immediate and clear — they didn't know what to do with it. The numbers didn't connect to anything they could act on.

The problem wasn't their data literacy. It was that I had designed the output for my own analytical process, not for their decision process. A collections rep doesn't need a normalized deviation score. They need to know: how many dollars are at risk on this account, how long has it been unresolved, and is this a pattern or a one-time thing.

I rebuilt the outputs. Rates became dollar amounts. Anomaly scores became plain-language flags: "duplicate payment — $4,200 unapplied," "short pay pattern — 12 invoices, average gap $340." The aging buckets mapped directly to the actions they already knew how to take. The result was that the same underlying analysis, re-expressed in their language, became immediately usable.

What I learned from that is that data literacy is not a fixed property of a person — it's a function of how well the data is presented relative to what that person is trying to do. The job of the data strategist is not to raise everyone's statistical sophistication. It's to build outputs that are native to the decisions being made, which requires understanding those decisions before you touch the data.

That same principle applied later in my research work, where I had to present identical findings differently to a technical thesis committee, a broad scientific conference audience, and the written record of a peer-reviewed journal. Different audiences, different entry points, same underlying analysis.

## Q4: What draws you to Sno-Isle Libraries, and how do you see data supporting public service and community impact?

I believe libraries are the clearest existing example of what cooperative public infrastructure looks like in practice. They are community-funded, universally accessible, non-commercial, and organized around a principle that information should be available to everyone regardless of economic status. That is not a neutral institutional posture — it is a commitment to a specific idea of what a community owes its members.

Safe access to information is not a feature of a well-functioning society. It is a precondition. And the degree to which that access is unevenly distributed — by geography, by language, by income, by disability — is the degree to which some people are excluded from full participation in their own communities. Libraries are one of the institutions that push back against that exclusion in a concrete, daily, unglamorous way.

That is what draws me to Sno-Isle specifically, not just libraries in the abstract. I have done analytic work in Snohomish County — mapping transit accessibility and vulnerability across the county's population — and I understand something of the geographic and demographic texture of this place. I know that the distance between rural Island County and an urban service center is not just miles. I know which populations are most likely to face barriers to access and why. Data has a role in making that visible and keeping it visible so that decisions don't drift toward serving people who are easiest to serve.

Data supports public service and community impact when it is used to ask honest questions: Who is being reached? Who isn't? What would it take to reach them? Those questions require both technical rigor and a clear sense of what equity actually means in practice — not just as a compliance category, but as a standard against which to measure whether the work is working.

I want to do that work at Sno-Isle because I live in this community, I believe in what libraries do, and I think data strategy done well is one of the most effective tools an organization has for staying accountable to its own mission.
