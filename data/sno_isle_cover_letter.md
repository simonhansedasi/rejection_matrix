# Cover Letter — Sno-Isle Libraries Data Strategist

---

My family uses Sno-Isle. My wife checks out novels. My toddler goes through picture books faster than we can return them. I built a command-line tool integrating the BiblioCommons API with a rating and checkout-count system so I could queue holds before we leave the house. I also use the library for my own research. I am developing a precision agriculture company, and Sno-Isle's access to journals, databases, and community resources is part of how I do that work. This is infrastructure I depend on.

That is also why I take seriously what libraries provide for people with fewer options. Not everyone has an internet connection at home. For someone without a job who cannot afford one, the library is often the only place to search for work and access the digital systems the rest of us take for granted. Libraries offer unconditional access to information and the tools to use it. That matters.

I encountered this firsthand in a way that shaped one of the projects I am most proud of. I met members of the local transit advocacy community at a library branch. Those conversations led me to build a transit accessibility and vulnerability analysis for Snohomish County. I integrated Census demographic data with GTFS transit feeds to map where service gaps overlapped with populations least able to absorb them: elderly residents, car-free households, people with limited English proficiency, low-income families. The library was the community connection that made that work happen.

My approach to data strategy is straightforward. The better a problem is understood, the more effectively it can be solved. At Randy's World-Wide, that meant forensically reconstructing what a predecessor had done before I could systematically clean it up. In transit vulnerability work, it meant asking not just where the gaps were but who was living inside them. At Sno-Isle, it would mean asking honestly who is being reached, who is not, and what the data can tell us about closing that distance.

I want to do this work here because I am already a patron. I know this geography. I know something of this population. My belief in what this institution does is what makes me want to apply my skills to it.

Simon-Hans Edasi
